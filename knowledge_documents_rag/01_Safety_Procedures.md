## FOREWORD

This manual sets out the safety procedures that govern work in the production areas of Plant 7. It is written for every person who enters the floor: operators, maintenance technicians, quality inspectors, supervisors, temporary workers, contractors, and visitors. Nothing in this manual is optional. The procedures described here exist because each one has, at some point, been the difference between a normal shift and a serious injury.

Floor supervisors hold a special responsibility under this manual. A supervisor is accountable for confirming, before any task begins, that the people performing it are trained for it, equipped for it, and that the equipment is in a safe condition. Where this manual and an equipment manufacturer's manual disagree, follow whichever is more protective of the worker.

If you are ever unsure whether a task is safe, stop. The authority to stop work for safety belongs to every employee in this plant and may never be overridden for the sake of the production schedule.

## SECTION 1 — GENERAL SAFETY RULES

All personnel must observe the following baseline rules at all times within production areas.

Safety glasses meeting ANSI Z87.1 with side shields and safety-toe footwear meeting ASTM F2413 are required the moment you cross onto the production floor, and remain required until you leave it. Additional protective equipment is required by area, as described in Section 2.

Horseplay, running (except in an emergency), and the use of personal audio earbuds are prohibited on the floor. Loose clothing, neckwear, jewelry, lanyards, and unsecured long hair must be removed or restrained before approaching any rotating or reciprocating machinery; these items are a primary cause of entanglement injuries.

Aisles, electrical panels, fire extinguishers, eyewash stations, and exits must be kept clear at all times. Never store material or park equipment in front of any of these.

Report every hazard, every injury, and every near-miss to your supervisor. Reporting is non-punitive. A hazard that is reported can be fixed; a hazard that is hidden injures the next person.

## SECTION 2 — PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT

Personal protective equipment, or PPE, is your last line of defense. It does not remove a hazard; it reduces the harm if every other control fails. PPE is mandatory by area, and the supervisor verifies compliance at the start of each shift.

### 2.1 Eye and Face Protection

Safety glasses are worn everywhere on the floor. In addition, a welding shield with the correct shade (10 to 13 depending on amperage) is required at the robotic welding cells, and a face shield over safety glasses is required when spraying in the powder coat booth and when handling the corrosive pretreatment solution.

### 2.2 Hearing Protection

Hearing protection — either plugs or muffs — is required wherever the noise level meets or exceeds 85 dBA measured as an eight-hour time-weighted average. Press Line A operates at approximately 92 dBA during stamping and always requires protection rated NRR 25 or higher. The CNC machining cells run between 84 and 88 dBA; protection rated NRR 22 or higher is required there. Every employee assigned to these areas is enrolled in the Hearing Conservation Program and receives annual audiometric testing.

### 2.3 Hand Protection

Hand protection is selected for the task. Cut-resistant gloves rated ANSI A4 are worn when handling stamped blanks and sheet stock at Press Line A. ANSI A2 gloves are worn in machining and general assembly. Leather welding gloves are worn at the welding cells. Chemical-resistant nitrile gloves are worn when handling coolants, oils, and finishing chemicals.

**WARNING:** Gloves must NOT be worn at the point of operation of the stamping press or while a CNC spindle is rotating. A glove caught in a die or spindle will pull the hand in with it. Remove gloves before reaching into these zones.

### 2.4 Foot, Body, and Respiratory Protection

Safety-toe footwear is the plant minimum. Metatarsal guard boots are required at Press Line A, where dropped dies and blanks are a hazard. Flame-resistant jackets and leather spats are required at the welding cells. In the powder coat booth, a Tyvek suit and at minimum an N95 half-face respirator are required because of the airborne powder.

The PPE requirements by area are summarized below for quick reference.

| Area | Eye | Hearing | Hand | Foot | Additional |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stamping — Press Line A | Z87.1 glasses | ≥ NRR 25 | Cut-resistant A4 | Metatarsal boots | No gloves at point of operation |
| CNC Machining Cells | Z87.1 glasses | ≥ NRR 22 | Cut-resistant A2 | Safety-toe | No gloves while spindle runs |
| Robotic Welding WC-1/WC-2 | Welding shield 10–13 | ≥ NRR 22 | Leather welding | Safety-toe, leather spats | FR jacket, no synthetics |
| Assembly | Z87.1 glasses | As posted | Nitrile or A2 | Safety-toe | — |
| Powder Coat Finishing | Glasses + face shield | ≥ NRR 22 | Chemical-resistant nitrile | Safety-toe | Respirator, Tyvek suit |

## SECTION 3 — CONTROL OF HAZARDOUS ENERGY (LOCKOUT/TAGOUT)

This section is the most important in the manual. The control of hazardous energy — lockout/tagout, or LOTO — prevents machinery from starting or releasing stored energy while someone is working on it.

### 3.1 When LOTO Is Required

You must apply lockout/tagout before performing any of the following: clearing a jam, changing a die or tooling, performing maintenance, removing or bypassing a guard, or placing any part of your body into a point of operation or other danger zone.

**WARNING:** No work is ever performed under a machine guard, inside a die area, or within the reach of a robot without lockout/tagout applied and verified. There are no exceptions to this rule, including for tasks that "will only take a second."

### 3.2 Energy Sources

Before you can isolate energy, you must know what energy is present. The principal sources for each major asset are listed below.

| Equipment | Electrical | Hydraulic | Pneumatic | Stored / Other |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MPS-400 Press (Line A) | 480V 3-phase | 2,200 psi main ram | 90 psi counterbalance | Flywheel inertia; gravity on ram and die |
| CNC VF-4 Cell | 480V 3-phase | — | 85 psi chuck/coolant | Spindle rotation; servo axes |
| Welding Cell WC-1/WC-2 | 480V 3-phase | — | 90 psi clamps | Robot arm gravity; shielding-gas pressure |
| Powder Coat Booth | 480V / 120V controls | — | 80 psi spray | Compressed gas; static charge |

### 3.3 The Lockout Procedure

Perform the following six steps in order. Do not skip or reorder any step.

**Step 1 — Notify.** Tell all affected employees that the equipment is being shut down and locked out, and why.

**Step 2 — Shut Down.** Stop the equipment using its normal stopping procedure. Do not simply pull the disconnect on a running machine.

**Step 3 — Isolate.** Operate each energy-isolating device to disconnect the equipment from its energy sources. Open the electrical disconnect. Close and lock the hydraulic and pneumatic supply valves.

**Step 4 — Apply Locks and Tags.** Each authorized employee applies their own assigned padlock to each isolating device, together with a tag showing their name, department, and the date. When more than one person works on the same equipment, use a group lockbox: each isolating device is locked, the keys are placed in the lockbox, and every worker applies a personal lock to the box.

**Step 5 — Release Stored Energy.** Dissipate or restrain all stored energy. Bleed hydraulic and pneumatic lines to zero pressure. Allow the press flywheel and any spindle to come to a complete mechanical stop. Discharge capacitors after waiting the manufacturer's specified time. On the press, insert the mechanical safety prop as described in 3.4.

**Step 6 — Verify.** Confirm the zero-energy state. Attempt to start the equipment using the normal controls — it must not start — then return the controls to the off position. Check that all pressure gauges read zero. Only now may work begin.

### 3.4 Press Ram Safety Prop

**CAUTION:** Electrical and hydraulic isolation alone will not prevent the ram of the MPS-400 from drifting downward under its own weight. Whenever any part of the body enters the die area, the mechanical safety prop MUST be inserted, even after lockout is applied. Confirm the prop is fully seated before reaching in.

### 3.5 Removal of Locks

Only the employee who applied a lock may remove it. If that employee has left the site and cannot be reached, their lock may be removed only by the area supervisor following the lock-removal verification procedure: confirm the employee is off-site, confirm the danger zone is clear of all personnel, document the removal, and notify the employee before their next shift.

## SECTION 4 — MACHINE GUARDING

Every point-of-operation guard, light curtain, two-hand control, and interlock must be in place and functioning before a machine is operated.

**WARNING:** Bypassing, defeating, taping over, or otherwise disabling a safety interlock or guard is grounds for immediate termination. It also endangers the next person to use the machine.

The MPS-400 press is protected by a Category 4 light curtain at the point of operation and by two-hand, anti-tie-down controls that require both of the operator's hands to be clear of the die. If the light curtain is interrupted during the downstroke, the press must stop within its calculated safety distance. The light curtain and brake-monitor stopping time are verified at the start of every shift; the press may not be run if either test fails.

The CNC VF-4 cells have interlocked enclosure doors. In production mode the spindle will not turn with the door open. The welding cells are surrounded by perimeter guarding with interlocked gates; the robot will only move at reduced speed when an operator is inside in teach mode and is holding the enabling device.

If you find a guard that is missing, damaged, or defeated, stop the machine, apply a red out-of-service tag, and notify your supervisor at once.

## SECTION 5 — HAZARDOUS CHEMICALS

A Safety Data Sheet (SDS) is available for every chemical on site, through the SDS terminal and in the binder at each area station. Read the SDS before using any chemical you are not already trained on, and follow the PPE and handling instructions it specifies.

The principal chemicals in use are way and spindle oil (ISO VG 68) and water-soluble cutting coolant in machining; epoxy powder, corrosive phosphate pretreatment, and solvents in finishing; and argon/CO₂ shielding gas at the welding cells.

**WARNING — Combustible Dust:** Powder coat overspray is a combustible dust. Never use compressed air to blow down powder accumulation, as this creates an explosive cloud. Clean only with non-sparking tools and explosion-rated vacuums. Booth filters are inspected weekly.

**WARNING — Asphyxiation:** Shielding gas displaces oxygen. Maintain ventilation at the welding cells and never enter a low or enclosed area where gas may have collected without atmospheric testing.

In the event of a small spill, use the spill kit at the area station, ventilate the area, and notify EHS. For a large spill, evacuate the immediate area and call for emergency response.

## SECTION 6 — HOT WORK

Welding inside the dedicated cells WC-1 and WC-2 is governed by standing procedure and does not require a separate permit. Any welding, cutting, or grinding performed anywhere else in the plant requires a Hot Work Permit issued by the EHS Coordinator or a supervisor and valid for one shift.

Before hot work begins, clear all combustibles within a 35-foot radius or shield them, place a fire extinguisher within reach, and assign a fire watch. The fire watch remains in place during the work and for 30 minutes after it is complete, and the area is re-inspected at the end of that period before the watch is released.

## SECTION 7 — POWERED INDUSTRIAL TRUCKS

Only certified operators may operate a forklift. Certification is renewed every three years and after any incident. The operator performs and logs a pre-operation inspection — forks, tires, horn, brakes, hydraulics, and leaks — before first use each shift.

The plant speed limit is 5 mph. Pedestrians have absolute right-of-way at marked crossings. Sound the horn at every blind corner and doorway. Never raise a person on the forks; use the approved work platform with the fork-mounted safety cage if elevated work is required.

## SECTION 8 — CONFINED SPACE ENTRY

Certain locations — coolant sump pits, the interior of the powder coat oven, and large material hoppers — are permit-required confined spaces. Entry into these spaces is never performed alone and never without a permit.

Confined-space entry requires atmospheric testing before and during entry (oxygen between 19.5 and 23.5 percent, combustible gas below 10 percent of the lower explosive limit, and toxic gases below their exposure limits), continuous monitoring, an attendant stationed outside the space, and a rescue and retrieval plan.

**NOTE:** Floor supervisors are not authorized to issue confined-space entry permits. All confined-space work must be escalated to the EHS Coordinator.

## SECTION 9 — EMERGENCY RESPONSE

### 9.1 Evacuation

When the continuous evacuation alarm sounds, shut down your equipment if it is safe to do so — press the emergency stop — and leave by the nearest marked exit. Proceed to the muster point in the Assembly Lot (the north parking row). Supervisors account for every member of their crew and report the headcount to the Incident Commander. Do not re-enter the building until the all-clear is given.

### 9.2 Reporting Emergencies

For any fire, serious medical emergency, or major chemical release, call 911 first, then notify EHS at extension 4400. For a minor injury, notify your supervisor and use the nearest first-aid station. For an equipment fault or power loss, notify Maintenance at extension 4500 and refer to manual MPM-MNT-002.

### 9.3 Injury and Near-Miss Reporting

Every injury — including minor first-aid cases — and every near-miss must be reported to the supervisor before the end of the shift in which it occurs. The supervisor completes the formal incident report within 24 hours. Near-miss reporting is encouraged and is never grounds for discipline; a near-miss reported today prevents an injury tomorrow.

---

## SECTION 10 — ERGONOMICS AND MANUAL HANDLING

Repetitive assembly tasks are rotated among crew members every two hours to reduce strain. Any manual lift over 40 pounds requires either a team lift or a mechanical aid such as a hoist, cart, or vacuum lifter. Stamped blanks and finished bracket assemblies (part number MPM-4471) are moved in containers on roller carts and are never carried in hand-held stacks.

---

## SECTION 11 — SUPERVISOR RESPONSIBILITIES

Before any shift or task, a floor supervisor confirms that the crew is trained and current, that required PPE is worn, that all guards and interlocks are functional, that lockout/tagout is applied for any servicing, and that the work area is orderly. During the shift, the supervisor stops any unsafe act immediately and escalates any condition beyond their authority — confined-space work, structural hazards, or repeated unsafe behavior — to EHS.

The supervisor leads by example. A crew watches what a supervisor permits far more closely than what a manual says.
